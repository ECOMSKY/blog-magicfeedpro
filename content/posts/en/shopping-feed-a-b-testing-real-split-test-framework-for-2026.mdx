Google shopping feed testing is one of the highest-leverage activities a performance team can run — yet most brands treat it as "push a change, watch the dashboard for a week, and gut-call the winner." That's not a test; it's noise. Shopify Plus teams running $50k+/month Shopping budgets have moved to a 3-cohort custom-label method that isolates feed variables with enough statistical power to attribute 8–15% CTR lifts per quarter to specific title and attribute changes — before touching the full catalog.

## Why Standard PMax 'Experiments' Miss Feed-Level Variables

Performance Max campaign-level experiments — the "Experiment" tab inside Google Ads — split budget between two campaign configurations. What they cannot do is isolate a **feed change** as the independent variable. When you modify a product title catalog-wide and then run a PMax experiment, you've already contaminated both arms: every impression now serves the new title, and the control group doesn't exist anymore.

Google's own [Merchant Center experiment documentation](https://support.google.com/merchants/) acknowledges this gap implicitly: the supported experiment types cover bidding, creative assets, and URL expansions — not feed attributes. That means a title rewrite, a brand-prefix change, or swapping material attributes into position 2 of a title string cannot be tested natively inside the Ads UI.

The consequence is real money left on the table. In a catalog of 2,400 SKUs, if you roll out a title format change based on anecdotal reasoning and it underperforms by 6% CTR, you've degraded every Shopping impression across that catalog with zero ability to reverse-attribute the drop. You might not even notice for 3–4 weeks — long enough for a quarterly review to paint a false picture. Before committing to any catalog-wide change, it pays to audit your feed for attribute gaps that could introduce confounding variables from day one.

### The Three Variables PMax Experiments Actually Control

PMax experiments handle: (1) creative asset groups, (2) Smart Bidding strategy variants (tROAS vs. Maximize Conversion Value), and (3) URL expansion toggles. Feed-level signals — titles, descriptions, product types, GTINs, custom labels — are upstream of the auction. They determine **which queries your ads are eligible to enter**, not just how you bid into them. Testing at the wrong layer answers the wrong question.

## The 3-Cohort Split Method: Segmenting by Custom Label

The 3-cohort custom-label method is the most reliable approach to isolating feed variables in a live Shopping environment. It uses three cohorts defined by `custom_label_0` (or whichever label slot is free in your feed). Label your SKU pool as `control`, `variant_a`, and `holdout` before touching any titles or attributes. The holdout group — typically 20% of SKUs — sits untouched and runs through the same campaigns without any modification, giving you a baseline that accounts for external seasonality shifts.

Here's the cohort allocation formula we've validated across multiple Shopify Plus accounts:

| Cohort | Label Value | SKU % | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Control | `test_ctrl` | 40% | Original feed attributes, business-as-usual |
| Variant A | `test_var_a` | 40% | Modified titles / attributes under test |
| Holdout | `test_hold` | 20% | Untouched; seasonality / market correction |

Assigning custom labels at scale requires a supplemental feed rather than editing your primary feed. In your Merchant Center account, create a supplemental feed mapped to `id` + `custom_label_0` only. This keeps your primary feed clean and lets you swap label values programmatically via the [Content API](https://support.google.com/merchants/) without a full feed re-upload.

Once labels are assigned, segment your Shopping or PMax campaigns by label using campaign-level product filters. Control and Variant A get identical budgets, identical bid strategies, and identical asset groups. The only variable that differs is what's in the feed. If you have more than one Shopping campaign, you need to handle cross-contamination — more on that in a later section.

## Setting Statistical Significance Thresholds (Sample Size Math)

Running a test for 14 days and declaring a winner based on a 3% CTR difference is how brands fool themselves. Before launching any feed test, calculate the minimum detectable effect (MDE) and required sample size using your baseline click volume. Getting this step right is what separates a defensible result from a gut-call dressed up in data.

The standard formula, derived from Evan Miller's [sample size calculator methodology](https://www.evanmiller.org/ab-testing/sample-size.html), targets:

- **Statistical power**: 80% (β = 0.20)
- **Significance level**: 95% (α = 0.05, two-tailed)
- **MDE**: the smallest CTR lift worth actioning (typically 5–8% relative for feed tests)

For a baseline CTR of 1.2% and an MDE of 6% relative (meaning you want to detect a lift to 1.27% or above), you need approximately **18,400 impressions per cohort**. At a typical Shopping campaign impression pace of 1,500 impressions/day per cohort at that budget tier, that's a 12-day minimum window — not 7, and not the "let's check Friday afternoon" habit most teams fall into.

<Callout type="warning">
Never end a feed test on a weekend or cut it short during a promotional period. Holiday weekends, flash sales, and even competitor promotions shift CTR baselines by 15–30%, invalidating the comparison. Pick a 14-day window that spans two full business weeks with no scheduled promotions on either side.
</Callout>

For teams with smaller catalogs (under 500 SKUs per cohort) or lower-traffic categories, the math often yields required windows of 21–28 days. That's uncomfortable but correct. Calling a test at day 10 with p = 0.08 is not "trending toward significance" — it's an underpowered test with a coin-flip conclusion.

The primary metric for feed tests should be **CTR at the impression level** (clicks ÷ impressions), not conversion rate. Conversion rate introduces downstream variables — landing page, price competitiveness, stock availability — that are outside the feed's control. Isolate the feed's job: getting the click.

## Case Study: Title Format Test Across 2,400 SKUs (14-Day Window)

A Shopify Plus apparel brand running approximately $65k/month on Google Shopping ran a title format test in Q1 2026 using the 3-cohort method described above. The test variable was title structure: **Control** used the default Shopify product title (`Brand + Product Name + Color`), while **Variant A** restructured to `Brand + Gender + Product Category + Key Attribute + Color` — a format that front-loads search-intent signals. This type of structured title rewrite is one of the highest-impact changes documented in product title optimization for Google Shopping.

Results after 14 days across 2,400 SKUs (800 per cohort):

| Metric | Control | Variant A | Lift |
|---|---|---|---|
| Impressions | 312,400 | 308,900 | — |
| Clicks | 3,748 | 4,271 | +13.9% |
| CTR | 1.20% | 1.38% | +15.0% |
| Conv. Rate | 2.14% | 2.19% | +2.3% (ns) |
| p-value | — | — | 0.003 |

The CTR lift of 15% cleared the 95% confidence threshold comfortably (p = 0.003). Conversion rate improvement was not statistically significant — which was expected, since the test only changed the feed, not the landing page. The team rolled out Variant A titles to the remaining 1,600 SKUs and saw the lift hold within 2 percentage points over the subsequent 30 days.

<Callout type="tip">
Publish your test design — cohort size, MDE, run length — before you start the experiment. Teams that pre-register their success criteria are significantly less likely to engage in "p-hacking" (stopping the test when the number looks good). A shared Google Sheet with the hypothesis, metrics, and threshold locked before day 1 takes under 20 minutes and pays for itself every time the result is ambiguous.
</Callout>

## Avoiding Cross-Contamination in Shared Shopping Campaigns

Cross-contamination is the most common reason feed tests fail silently. It happens when control and variant SKUs compete in the same ad group or when Google's Smart Bidding algorithm redistributes budget toward whichever cohort appears to be performing better mid-test. Eliminating these vectors before launch is non-negotiable.

Three specific contamination vectors to eliminate before launch:

**1. Shared ad groups.** If both cohorts live inside the same ad group, Google will auto-prioritize the higher-CTR cohort as the test progresses, inflating the variant's impression share at the control's expense. The custom label filter at the campaign level (not ad group level) is the structural fix.

**2. Smart Bidding learning bleed.** tROAS and Maximize Conversion Value strategies share a performance signal pool across campaigns in the same account. A bid strategy change triggered by Variant A performance can bleed into Control campaign bidding behavior within 48–72 hours. Use separate, independent bid strategies for each cohort — even if that means identical tROAS targets duplicated across two campaigns.

**3. Remarketing list overlap.** If your Shopping campaigns use audience signals that overlap between cohorts (common with broad first-party lists), users who saw Control ads may be retargeted by Variant A impressions, mixing exposure data. Segment your RLSA audiences by purchase recency and exclude the top-recency segments from both test campaigns during the run window.

For accounts with complex multi-campaign structures, Shopify's [Google Shopping channel documentation](https://help.shopify.com/en/manual/online-sales-channels/google) covers how supplemental feed architectures interact with campaign product filters — useful background when designing label hierarchies across 10+ active campaigns.

## Tooling: Google Sheets Template + Merchant Center API Workflow

A reliable feed test lives or dies by its operational scaffolding. Manual label assignment across thousands of SKUs is error-prone; so is manually checking significance each morning. Here's the minimal tooling stack that scales without a data engineering team.

**Cohort assignment (Google Sheets + IMPORTDATA):** Maintain a master SKU list with a `cohort` column. Use `=RANDBETWEEN(1,100)` at initial assignment with a cutoff (1–40 = control, 41–80 = variant, 81–100 = holdout) — but critically, **paste values immediately** after generation to freeze the random assignment. Cohorts that regenerate on every sheet open produce different assignments each day, corrupting the test.

**Supplemental feed (Merchant Center Content API):** Use the [Merchant Center Content API](https://support.google.com/merchants/) to push `custom_label_0` updates from your Sheets assignment via a lightweight Apps Script or Python script. This avoids the 24-hour feed processing lag associated with manual supplemental feed uploads and gives you near-real-time label control — critical when you need to pause a cohort due to an anomaly.

**Significance tracking (Apps Script + evanmiller.org formula):** Replicate the two-proportion z-test formula directly in Sheets. Pull daily impression and click data from the Google Ads API into a running table. The z-test formula for two proportions:

```
z = (p1 - p2) / sqrt(p_pool * (1 - p_pool) * (1/n1 + 1/n2))
```

where `p_pool = (x1 + x2) / (n1 + n2)`. Flag the p-value column red when p > 0.05 and green when p ≤ 0.05 — a two-second visual check replaces a morning of manual analysis.

The entire workflow — Sheets template, Apps Script for API sync, and significance tracker — can be set up in approximately 3 hours for a team that has API access already configured. That's a one-time investment that makes every subsequent test faster and more defensible to stakeholders who want to see the math before approving a catalog-wide rollout.
